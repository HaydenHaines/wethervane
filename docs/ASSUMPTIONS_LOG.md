# Assumptions Log

Every assumption the US Political Covariation Model makes is recorded here. Assumptions are living documents: they should be tested, revised, or falsified as work progresses.

## How to use this log

- **Add** a new row whenever a modeling or data decision rests on an untested belief.
- **Update Status** as evidence accumulates: Active --> Tested --> Revised or Falsified.
- **Link** to notebooks, ADRs, or issues that provide evidence for or against each assumption.

|----|-----------|----------|----------|--------|----------------|-------------|
| A001 | Voters are stickier than polls suggest -- most apparent "swing" is differential turnout and composition change, not large-scale persuasion. | Voter behavior | Gelman et al. 2016 ("The Mythical Swing Voter"); Kalla & Broockman 2018 (minimal persuasion effects in field experiments); ANES panel re-interview data showing high individual stability. | Active -- supported by literature | If wrong, the covariance structure would need to accommodate larger persuasion swings per type, and the turnout/persuasion decomposition loses its main prior. The model would still work but priors on type-level shift magnitudes would need to be wider. | Compare hindcast accuracy with strong-stability prior vs. weak prior. Check whether relaxing the stability assumption improves or degrades out-of-sample fit. |
| A002 | Community-level covariance is stable across 2-3 election cycles (roughly 6-12 years). | Covariance stability | PCA of county-level presidential returns (2008-2020) shows the first 3-4 principal components are largely stable across cycles. Factor loadings rotate slowly. | Active -- to test | If covariance drifts rapidly, the model must re-estimate structure each cycle and loses the ability to propagate early-cycle polls through historical covariance. The "memory" of the model shrinks. | Compute rolling-window PCA on type-level results. Measure subspace angle between factor spaces across consecutive cycles. Flag if angle exceeds threshold (e.g., > 15 degrees for top-3 subspace). |
| A003 | Communities cross administrative borders in politically meaningful ways -- county/state lines do not fully partition community structure. | Community structure | Bailey et al. 2018 Social Connectedness Index (SCI) clustering shows social networks span state lines. North FL / South GA / South AL share economic and religious community patterns despite state boundaries. | Active -- to validate | If communities are effectively contained within states, the cross-border propagation value of the model is reduced, though the within-state community typing still adds value. The FL+GA+AL proof-of-concept region would still work but the cross-border test would be less compelling. | Build community detection with and without cross-border edges. Compare type assignments and prediction accuracy at border counties. Measure whether cross-border community membership improves holdout prediction. |
| A004 | "Who you talk to daily" -- shaped by religion, class, neighborhood, and workplace -- is the right operationalization of community for political covariance. | Community definition | Theoretical basis in Huckfeldt & Sprague (1995) "Citizens, Politics, and Social Communication"; Mutz (2006) on cross-cutting exposure; social influence literature. The claim is that daily-interaction networks are the mechanism through which political norms are maintained and shifted. | Active -- assumption | If the relevant community is defined by media consumption, online networks, or identity rather than local interaction, the geographic community typing will miss key structure. The model would underperform in areas where media/online communities dominate local ones. | Compare community types derived from interaction proxies (commuting, religion, SCI) vs. media-market-based or demographic-only types. Check which produces better political covariance structure. |
| A005 | Soft community-type assignments (counties as mixtures of types) are more accurate than hard assignments (each county = exactly one type). | Methodology | Counties are heterogeneous -- e.g., a county may be 40% evangelical rural, 30% military/retiree, 30% suburban professional. Hard assignment forces a single label and loses within-county variation. NMF and topic model literature supports mixture representations. | Active -- to compare | If hard assignments are sufficient, the model simplifies considerably: no spectral unmixing needed for polls, simpler aggregation math. Would reduce complexity but potentially at the cost of accuracy in heterogeneous counties. | Run both hard (Leiden/SBM cluster labels) and soft (NMF weights) pipelines. Compare hindcast accuracy, especially in large or heterogeneous counties. Measure residual spatial autocorrelation under each. |
| A006 | 3-5 latent factors capture most of the political covariance structure across community types. | Dimensionality | Prior PCA on county-level presidential results typically finds 2-4 factors explaining 85-95% of variance. Adding more factors yields diminishing returns and risks overfitting with limited election cycles. | Active -- to test | If more factors are needed, the model requires more elections to estimate them reliably, worsening the N_types >> N_elections problem. If fewer suffice, the model simplifies. | Scree plot and cross-validated factor analysis on type-level election results. Compare BIC/WAIC across factor counts. Check whether additional factors improve out-of-sample prediction. |
| A007 | Turnout and vote share have a meaningful joint covariance worth modeling -- they are not independent conditional on community type. | Model structure | Empirically, type-level turnout shifts and vote-share shifts are correlated (e.g., higher Black turnout in 2008/2012 co-occurred with higher Democratic vote share in those communities). Modeling them jointly should improve estimates of both. | Active -- to test | If turnout and share are approximately independent given type, the dual-output model adds complexity without benefit. Could simplify to separate models. | Estimate joint vs. independent models on historical data. Compare WAIC/LOO. Test whether joint model produces better county-level predictions than independent models. |
| A008 | The spectral unmixing / linear mixing model adequately describes poll decomposition -- a poll's result can be approximated as a weighted sum of community-type opinions, weighted by the poll's geographic/demographic footprint. | Methodology | Linear mixing is standard in remote sensing and has been adapted for opinion aggregation. The assumption is that nonlinear interaction effects between types within a poll's sample are small relative to the linear combination signal. | Active -- assumption | If poll responses involve strong nonlinear interaction (e.g., social desirability effects that vary by local type composition), the unmixing will be biased. Would need a more complex poll model. | Simulate polls from known type-level opinions with and without interaction effects. Measure recovery accuracy of the linear unmixing. Compare to nonlinear alternatives on real polls where ground truth is approximated by election results. |
| A009 | FL+GA+AL has sufficient community diversity for proof of concept -- the region contains enough distinct community types to demonstrate the model's value. | Scope | Florida alone has Cuban, Puerto Rican, Haitian, retiree, military, evangelical, rural Southern, suburban professional, and university communities. Georgia adds Black Belt, Atlanta suburbs, and rural Appalachian foothills. Alabama provides a safe-state anchor. 226 counties total. | Active | If the region lacks key community types found elsewhere (e.g., Rust Belt industrial, Great Plains agricultural, Pacific Coast tech), the model may work locally but not generalize. The proof of concept would be valid but incomplete. | After building the model, check type coverage against a national community typology. Identify types present in other regions but absent from FL+GA+AL. Plan expansion accordingly. |
| A010 | Free public data provides sufficient signal without commercial data or voter files for the MVP. | Data | Census ACS, MEDSL election returns, RCMS religious data, IRS migration, LODES commuting, and SCI are all freely available. Voter files add individual-level detail but are costly and have privacy constraints. | Active -- for MVP | If free data lacks the resolution to distinguish community types (e.g., cannot separate Cuban from Puerto Rican without voter-file ethnicity detail), the model may produce overly coarse types. Florida voter files are cheap and accessible as a fallback. | Build the full pipeline on free data first. Measure type distinctiveness and prediction accuracy. If insufficient, add Florida voter file (available for ~$0.10/record or free via academic agreements) and measure marginal improvement. |
| A011 | 2022 ACS demographic features (used for K=7 NMF community type assignment) adequately represent 2016 and 2018 tract demographics for historical covariance estimation. | Data / temporal consistency | Two sub-issues: (1) Tract boundary changes 2010→2020 — handled by using 2022 TIGER for all election years' spatial joins, so all elections consistently map to 2020-vintage tract geoids matching the 2022 ACS. (2) ACS vintage mismatch — using 2022 demographics to characterize 2016/2018 tracts is an approximation. K=7 community types are dominated by racial/ethnic composition (c2=Black urban, c4=Asian, c6=Hispanic), which is relatively stable over 6 years. Larger drift risk for rapidly gentrifying tracts. | **Superseded by shift-based discovery** -- See ADR-005 | NMF-based assumption is no longer primary path. Shift-based approach removes this risk by discovering communities from electoral shift patterns, then overlaying demographics descriptively. | Historical assumption retained for reference and comparison only. |
| A012 | Spatially contiguous communities (Queen contiguity) adequately capture political community structure. Tracts that share edges or corners are more likely to shift together politically than distant tracts. | Community structure | Spatial autocorrelation in election returns is well-established. Queen contiguity (edge or corner adjacency) is a standard constraint in spatial clustering and enforces geographic coherence. | Active -- to test | If political communities regularly span non-adjacent geographic areas (e.g., separated by state lines or large geographic barriers), spatial contiguity may be too restrictive. Communities defined purely by shift similarity without spatial constraint may yield different structure. | Compare HAC results with and without spatial contiguity. Measure whether spatially contiguous communities have more uniform demographic/economic composition than non-contiguous alternatives. Check prediction accuracy of both approaches on temporal holdout. |
| A013 | Nine-dimensional shift vectors (D%, R%, turnout changes across 2016→2020 pres, 2020→2024 pres, 2018→2022 midterm) capture sufficient electoral signal for community discovery without additional election cycles. | Methodology / data | Three election pairs yield 9 dimensions; each pair contributes 3 shifts (D%, R%, turnout). This is more than the dimensionality of community types (typically 3-5 per prior PCA studies). | Active -- to test | If community structure requires more election dimensions (e.g., distinct behavior in off-year vs. presidential cycles), 9-dim vectors may be insufficient. If fewer dimensions suffice, the discovery process may be more robust. | Perform PCA on shift vectors. Check scree plot and cumulative variance explained. Compare communities discovered from subsets of 3 vs. all 9 shifts. |
| A014 | Temporal holdout validation (train on pre-2024 shifts, test on 2024 shifts) provides adequate falsifiability. Communities that predict historical shifts well but fail to predict 2024 shifts are declared invalid. | Methodology | Temporal splits are standard in time-series validation. Shift-based communities inherit the temporal structure of the election data. | Active -- foundational to shift-based approach | If discovered communities are heavily data-driven artifacts of the 2016-2024 period, they may not generalize to 2026 and beyond. Testing on 2024 provides one hold-out cycle but does not guarantee robustness. | After 2026 and 2028 elections, retrain and compare 2024-discovered communities to 2026/2028 actual shifts. Report generalization error. |
